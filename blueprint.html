SHEET 01 / 19

I. The Refined Thesis

Enterprise AI has gotten very good at individual productivity — drafting, summarizing, retrieving. In 2026, the largest platforms (Microsoft Copilot, Viva, Teams agents) have also started using the word "execution" — shipping meeting agents that track deadlines, assign tasks, and route approvals. This is not a blind spot anymore. It's a race.

What none of these platforms do yet is treat execution as deviation from an organization's own stated plan, and — critically — learn from every deviation whether the plan or the prediction was wrong, and get better calibrated over time. They track tasks. They don't build organizational memory.

The actual bet: not "AI captures commitments" (increasingly commoditized), but AI accumulates, tests, and revises judgment about how this specific organization executes — judgment that compounds and gets more accurate every cycle, survives leadership turnover, and cannot be replicated by a generic agent layered on top of Teams or Slack.

Everything in this document is architected around protecting and operationalizing that one differentiator. Where a capability is closer to what incumbents already ship, we say so plainly — the honest wedge is narrower, and the design is built to defend it.

SHEET 04 / 19

IV. Capture — resolves the "noise" problem

Principle: commitments are captured at deliberate structured moments, not scraped from ambient conversation.
  • Each initiative/review cadence (e.g., weekly collections review) has a dedicated EI channel, not general Slack/Teams monitoring.
  • Either the individual owner types their own commitment in natural language ("ABC Industries — ₹1.5Cr by Friday"), or a scribe/PMO enters on their behalf — but every entry is explicitly tagged to an accountable owner.
  • Because the channel and participant list define intent, there's no ambiguity about whether a message is a commitment — this sidesteps the extraction/disambiguation problem that plagues generic "AI listens to all your chat" approaches.
  • When a scribe enters on someone's behalf, the bot echoes the captured commitment back for confirmation before logging — catching mis-tagging in the room, not three weeks later.
Dependency: this only works if the AOP/KPI target structure already exists in the Knowledge Graph before the meeting starts. Capture without a taught target to roll up against is just a chat log.

SHEET 05 / 19

V. Tolerance & Materiality — resolves "who decides deviation matters"

Principle: materiality thresholds are configured by the organization upfront, not inferred by the model — explainable and revisable, not a black box.
  • Every KPI/initiative class gets a tolerance band set during onboarding (e.g., "10% deviation from prior baseline") — not a single global number.
  • Bands are tiered by data maturity:
    • Mature, high-history initiatives (e.g., a 5-year-old business unit) → tight, numeric tolerance.
    • New/low-history initiatives (e.g., a market entry with one data point) → wide or qualitative tolerance, since there's no reliable base rate yet.
  • Thresholds are explicit and auditable: if EI flags something, the org can trace exactly why ("this initiative class has a 10% band, deviation was 14%") rather than trusting an opaque judgment call.
  • Thresholds are meant to be revised over time based on false-positive/false-negative rate — not fixed forever at onboarding.

SHEET 06 / 19

VI. Accountability & Ground Truth — resolves the honesty/incentive problem

Principle: the system does not depend on anyone volunteering bad news. Silence is itself a signal, and self-reports are checked against system-of-record data.
  • Default-to-unmet: no update by checkpoint → treated as not met, escalates automatically. Same consequence as an honest "delayed" update, minus the explanation. Silence is strictly worse than an early, explained flag.
  • Structured status only — on-track / delayed / blocked — preventing non-answers like "following up" from sitting in an undefined middle state.
  • Ground-truth reconciliation: self-reported status is a leading signal, not the only one. EI reconciles against actual transactional data (e.g., collections received in the books, tagged to invoice/reference) as a lagging confirmation. A gamed self-report still gets caught once reconciliation runs.

Net effect: the system converts "can someone avoid ever being caught" (no) into "can someone delay a conversation by a few days" (bounded, tolerable) — a materially better incentive structure than an honesty-dependent mandate model.

SHEET 07 / 19

VII. The Learning Loop The core bet

Principle: EI doesn't just accumulate patterns, it tests them. Overrides of history are tracked, scored against what actually happened, and promoted into the model or reverted — this is what "learning" has to mean for the compounding claim to be real, not just a pattern-library management manually argues around every cycle.
  1. EI surfaces a historical pattern during planning (e.g., "credit policy rollouts historically achieve 18% adoption vs. plan").
  2. Management can override, citing a specific reason the pattern doesn't apply this time (e.g., "this rollout has dedicated team + new tooling").
  3. EI records the override as a conditional hypothesis, not a permanent exception: "credit policy rollouts underperform historically, EXCEPT when paired with [dedicated team + new tooling] — untested."
  4. EI tracks the agreed leading indicators through the cycle.
  5. At cycle close, EI scores the override: did this time's outcome validate the stated condition, or did the historical base rate reassert itself?
  6. Promotion or reversion: validated → the conditional rule strengthens and becomes precedent for future similar overrides. Not validated → EI reverts to the original base rate, and says so explicitly next time.
Confidence-weighting: a single validated override should not immediately become settled truth. EI should carry and surface a confidence level on any conditional rule until there's enough repetition to be reasonably sure the cited condition — not some other confound — is what drove the different outcome.

This is the mechanism that makes "organizational memory compounds" an actual claim rather than a slogan — and it's the piece with no visible precedent in market today.

VIII. Honest Positioning

  • Not competing with ERPs (SAP/Oracle) on their own turf. They track completed transactions; no native object exists for a forward-looking, unconfirmed commitment. Real gap, but not where the interesting competitive risk sits.
  • The real competitive attention should be on Microsoft. As of 2026, Copilot is explicitly repositioning as an "execution layer" (Agent 365, Copilot Cowork), and Teams already has Facilitator, Channel Agents, and a Project Manager Agent doing deadline-tracking and task assignment inside chat.
  • What Microsoft has not shown: AOP-taught structured context as the backbone for deviation tracking, and — most importantly — a self-correcting learning loop that revises its own historical judgment based on tracked override outcomes. Their own OKR product (Viva Goals) was retired — weak evidence that structured goal-linkage is harder to land than generic task-tracking, not that it's solved.
Implication for strategy: the window to prove the AOP-linkage + learning-loop wedge is real but likely closing, not open-ended. This argues for a fast, narrow POC over a long, full-blueprint build — speed matters more than completeness right now.

SHEET 11 / 19

XI. Source of Truth & Target Discipline

Session 2 addition. Resolves a specific risk: EI computing its own version of "actual" and quietly disagreeing with the org's BI tool.

Rule: EI never independently computes actuals. It reads exactly what BI reads — same warehouse, same semantic layer, same numbers a finance analyst already trusts. If a metric is BI-derived (e.g. receivable days from outstanding ÷ daily sales), EI reads the computed metric, never reimplements the formula.

This produces three distinct numbers per KPI, each with a different owner and source:

  • Target — the frozen AOP baseline. Set once at ingestion. Never edited. EI's own Knowledge Graph is the system of record for this — it never existed in the ERP.
  • Committed Total — the live sum of individual commitments captured through trackers. Can land above, at, or below Target — e.g. a leader pushes for more and the team commits above-floor. No formal "revise target" workflow exists or is needed; the stretch is an emergent property of Capture, not a system-of-record event.
  • Actual — read from BI/warehouse. Ground truth, never computed independently.

EI's differentiated value is explicitly not a second analytics engine. BI shows the gap between Target and Actual. Only EI shows who owns closing it, and what's been promised toward it.

Correction ≠ Revision

"Target is frozen" holds for revision — the plan changing mid-cycle because ambition or reality shifts. That case is already resolved and deliberately has no edit mechanism: it surfaces as Committed Total exceeding Target, nothing about the baseline moves.

Correction is different in kind, not degree: the original entry was simply wrong — a mis-tagged owner, a transcription error, an initiative the parser missed or merged incorrectly. Not the plan changing; fixing the record of what was actually decided. A narrow, audited exception:

  • A deliberate, separate action — "Request a correction," re-opening the original confirm screen — never a casual inline edit on a live number.
  • Logged in the Context Ledger as its own event type (correction, distinct from commitment or a revision event) — required reason field, timestamped, never silent.
  • If sub-targets have already cascaded and people are committing against the old figure, the correction flags those downstream nodes for reconfirmation rather than leaving child commitments pointed at a number that no longer exists.

Net effect: the frozen-baseline trust property holds for the ordinary case — nobody casually drifts the number they're measured against — while Finance retains an honest, narrow path to fix a genuine mistake.

SHEET 12 / 19

XII. AOP Custodianship & Cascading

Corporate Finance is the AOP custodian — they upload the source plan, confirm Intake's parsed structure, and own the top-level cascade. This is a role clarification with real architectural consequences.

Visibility ≠ Accountability

Finance sees down to BU-level financials (and has visibility one layer below — client mix, existing vs. new) but is not accountable for it. A BU head's individual rep missing target is not Finance's action item unless it moves EBITDA materially. Visibility depth and accountability depth are separate properties on the Graph — a node can be visible to a role without that role owning escalations on it. RBAC needs to encode both dimensions, not one.

The Cascade Mechanism

Finance uploads whatever granularity genuinely exists — usually just BU-level. The system then pushes an empty-target workflow trigger down: "your number is X, fill in how you get there." If the source doc already has next-level detail, the system pre-populates it and the recipient confirms rather than starting blank. This repeats recursively until an owner declares "the buck stops with me" — a leaf node, cascade terminates. Depth is not uniform across branches (Sales may cascade five layers deep via a funnel model; Compliance may be one layer, owner to pass/fail).

Two independent checks fire at cascade time, not one: a consistency check (do sub-numbers sum to the parent's number — pure arithmetic, no history needed) and a plausibility check (does this sub-target look achievable given history — the Sheet VII override mechanism, triggered earlier in the cycle).

SHEET 13 / 19

XIII. Cadence & Adaptive Follow-Up

Review cadence is a property set per KPI node at ingestion — weekly collections, fortnightly pipeline, daily ops metrics, monthly for a different BU on the same metric. Never a global timer. This cadence property is what licenses Action's nudging: a node is only checked and nudged at its own configured rhythm.

Cadence and calendar sync are deliberately not the same toggle — people combine multiple KPIs into one meeting, and per-KPI calendar invites don't survive that reality. Calendar integration, if built at all, stays a separate optional layer. ERP-reconciliation frequency is also tracked as its own cadence property, independent of nudge frequency — a daily internal check-in doesn't require daily ERP reconciliation.

Nudge Content Branches on Data Availability

Reconciled metricNudge states the computed gap directly — "We were supposed to be at ₹60Cr, we're ₹5Cr short — what's your plan?" Closer to Judgment doing its job than a generic ask.
Non-reconciled metricNudge is an open status request — "How are we doing on pipeline, can you share an update?" Nothing to compute a gap against yet.

Reconciliation sources are pluggable, not hardcoded to ERP. CRM (for pipeline-type metrics) slots into the same mechanism once integrated — same branching logic, different upstream source.

Plausibility Checks Have a Maturity Curve

Year one, cold-start: plausibility checks run against prior-year financial actuals already in the warehouse — "you grew 10% last year, this year implies 30%, intentional?" Works from cycle one, no bootstrap needed. From cycle two onward, checks sharpen using EI's own Ledger — commitment-level history, override outcomes, deal-cycle patterns — data no other system in the org has ever captured. Honest framing for a buyer: useful immediately, compounds every cycle after.

SHEET 14 / 19

XIV. The Commitment Tracker as a First-Class Object

A tracker is not a chat thread — it's a structured object: {tracker_id, linked_kpi_node, created_by, trigger_type, participants, status}. Binding to exactly one KPI node is mandatory at creation — without it, a typed commitment is an orphaned message, not a structured signal.

Two Trigger Types, One Mechanism

Cadence-triggered (automatic, per Sheet XIII) and ad-hoc (a manager sees a shortfall on their passive dashboard, EI offers "want to call a review?", manager hits "open tracker") both instantiate the identical underlying capture object. Ad-hoc trackers are explicitly not automated escalation — EI never auto-pings a manager that a report "isn't answering." It offers a tool; the manager decides.

Async by default. Nothing in Capture assumes synchronous presence — a live meeting and a persistent async thread are the same mechanism at different paces.

Participants & Permissions

  • Default participant list auto-populates from Graph ownership (level-1 under the linked KPI) — creator can add anyone else manually.
  • Every commitment typed in, regardless of who typed it, gets tagged and tracked identically — no second-class commitment type based on how someone joined.
  • Three tiers, not two: Owner, Member, Viewer. Owner (tracker creator) is the only one who can close it. Members can type commitments, contributing to the rollup. Viewers can see the thread and numbers but cannot contribute — read-only.
  • Two independent paths to visibility. Automatic: anyone who outranks the tracker within the Graph hierarchy can always see it — no action needed from the owner. Explicit: the owner can additionally tag anyone, regardless of Graph position, as a Viewer — a peer BU head, someone from Finance, a board member — extending visibility beyond the org chart without granting contribution rights.
  • View is filtered Owner/Member × Active/Closed — a personal inbox structure, not one global list.

Dependencies — a Distinct Object Type

A dependency ("I can hit this, provided IT delivers X by Friday") is not a smaller commitment — it's a blocking condition bound to a commitment, not directly to a KPI. It doesn't add to the rollup; it either clears or doesn't. Schema: {dependency_id, parent_commitment, responsible_owner, status, deadline}. A breached dependency deadline flags the parent commitment as at-risk without blaming its owner, and routes through the same escalation mechanism as a breached commitment — one mechanism, two feeding object types.

Lifecycle: Active → Closed → Archived

Closed (owner-only action): stops new commitments, freezes the rollup as the official number for that cycle. Independent of ERP reconciliation, which runs on its own cadence regardless of tracker state. Archived: cosmetic, per-user, no data mutation — declutters an individual's view without an owner gate. No reopening. A shortfall that resurfaces after closing starts a new tracker against the same (permanent) KPI node, optionally linked back to the tracker it supersedes — preserving a visible thread of history on that KPI rather than isolated, contextless episodes.

Notification Model

Badge count increments only on a specific unresolved action owed by that user (an unanswered cadence question, a breached dependency deadline, a pending approval) — never on merely being a participant. Decrements only when that specific action is taken, not on opening/viewing. The badge is a UI surface on data Judgment/Action already track internally — no new backend logic, one query: every open item where this user is the responsible party and hasn't acted.

UI Pattern — Tabs, Not a Flat List

The tracker panel surfaces as tabs across the top of the workspace — Active (3), Closed (5) — each count reflecting that user's own Owner/Member-filtered view (Sheet XIV, above), with a notification glyph on any tab containing an item awaiting this user's action. Clicking a tab expands its thread list, each thread showing its own pending-item indicator inline. Clicking a thread opens that specific tracker. Mail-inbox conventions, deliberately — nothing novel to learn.

SHEET 15 / 19

XV. Build Discipline — Irreducible Core vs. Deferrable

Working checkpoint from Session 2: does this design still collapse to "BI + a diligent human" if oversimplified? Test every component against removal — if removing it doesn't change the outcome, it's deferrable, not core.

Irreducible — remove any of these and it stops being EI:
  • Commitment capture bound to a KPI node
  • Cadence-triggered nudging
  • Deviation-aware messaging when a reconciliation source exists
  • Breach-of-tolerance approval routing
  • Target vs. Committed vs. Actual, visibly and separately tracked
This set alone clears the bar. It nudges automatically, states the gap without being asked, and forces a routed decision on breach — none of which "BI plus someone diligent" does unprompted.

Genuinely deferrable — real value, not what draws the line:

  • Memory / pattern-learning against history (Sheet VII) — the compounding differentiator, but the irreducible set delivers value before Memory has cycle data to work with
  • Full recursive multi-level auto-cascading (Sheet XII) — v1 can have Finance manually seed the first one or two levels
  • CRM reconciliation, multi-system MCP integration — a single ERP connection proves the mechanism
  • Fine-grained RBAC tiers — a coarse two-tier version (owner sees own, manager sees team) covers most of the value
  • Dependency objects, tracker supersession linking, notification badging (Sheet XIV) — genuine UX quality, not load-bearing for the core loop

Everything discussed in Session 2 held together as an extension of the original mechanism — no primitive had to be invented to answer a hard case (shared source of truth, rolling targets, variable cadence, non-shaming escalation, async capture). That consistency is itself a signal the concept is sound; the open question is sequencing the build, not the design.

SHEET 16 / 19

XVI. Persistence, Roll-Up & Multi-Tracker Coordination

Node State Is Persistent. Trackers Are Episodic.

These are deliberately two different things, not one. A KPI node (Target, Committed Total, Actual, RAG status) is always live and queryable, all year — updated continuously by whatever last touched it: the most recent reconciliation run, or the most recently active tracker. A tracker stays exactly as Sheet XIV defines it: created, runs its course, closes, done. Nothing runs forever except the background scheduler — the always-on process that watches every node's cadence and automatically spins up a fresh tracker instance each time cadence comes due (Week 26's collections review, then Week 27's — a new instance each cycle, never a reopening of the last one).

Roll-Up Across Parent/Child Nodes: Linked vs. Manual Rows

When a tracker opens on a parent node (e.g. Company Q2 Revenue, 300M) and a child node feeding it (e.g. BU1's slice, 60M) already has its own active tracker, the system offers to link rather than forcing either duplicate entry or a full merge:

  • Link accepted — the parent tracker gets a single row for that BU, not a row per individual team member. The row is a live reference to the child tracker's own rollup, moving in real time as the child tracker collects more commitments, never manually retyped. Double-click drills into the child tracker to see the underlying individual commitments — read-only unless the viewer is separately added there.
  • Link declined — the two trackers stay fully independent. The child tracker's owner behaves like any other participant in the parent tracker: types a number by hand, a static snapshot that won't move again unless manually updated.
Link, never merge. Merging would force shared ownership — unclear who can close the thread. Linking keeps both trackers independently owned and closeable, with one tracker's live number simply made visible inside the other. A tracker row now has two flavors: a typed commitment (static until updated) and a linked reference (always current, drillable) — same visual row, different mechanism underneath.

Duplicate Trackers on the Same Node

If someone attempts to start a new tracker on a node that already has an active one, the system does not create a second, competing thread. It surfaces the existing tracker instead, gated by the same two visibility paths from Sheet XIV — automatic (Graph hierarchy) or explicit (owner-tagged Viewer). Visibility here means seeing, not controlling — someone senior surfacing an existing tracker this way does not gain close rights or the ability to act on the owner's behalf. If they want to actually act on the parent-level number, their path is the roll-up/linking mechanism above, not a takeover of the child tracker.

XVII. New Joiner Context — Push + Pull, One Capability

How does a new hire — at any level, Head of Servicing or an L2 individual contributor — get real org context, not just a role and a login? Resolved as one underlying capability with two entry points, not two separate systems.

The Underlying Capability Already Exists

The AI Assistant lens (Level 2.1, Tile 2 — the three-lens workspace) is a chat interface capable of answering context and history questions using the Knowledge Graph, Context Ledger, and Memory's scored patterns as its data source. No special "onboarding mode" or intent-detection is needed — a new hire's question and a ten-year veteran's question ("how does this team typically perform on collections?") get answered by the identical mechanism. The system doesn't need to know "this person is new" to answer well; it only needs to be genuinely good at answering that category of question at all.

Pull — Available From Day One, No Special Case

Open-ended natural-language query, any time, indefinitely. This is where most of the real value actually lands — the "why does X work this way" questions don't all surface on day one, they come up for months.

Push — Solves the "Don't Know What to Ask Yet" Problem

A real gap pull alone doesn't solve: a brand-new hire doesn't know the assistant exists, or what's worth asking. Push is a proactive trigger firing the same underlying capability, not separate content:

  • Account creation (role + level + team) places the person on a specific Graph node — enough on its own for EI to auto-assemble a starter briefing: what this role is accountable for, live initiatives underneath it, this team's historical performance, common failure patterns.
  • The manager's role is triggering the send, optionally layering a personal note — not authoring the content. Same pattern as Intake: system auto-populates, human confirms or adds, never starts from a blank page.
  • Scoped by the same existing visibility rules from Sheets XII and XIV — an L2 IC gets a narrower slice than a Head of Servicing automatically, because visibility is already Graph-position-based. No new rule required, just applied to a new trigger event (account creation) rather than only tracker access.
  • Re-triggerable, not a one-time artifact — the briefing is the assistant capability fired proactively, so a person can ask for it again weeks later once they have enough context to make better sense of it, the same as any other query.
This is the literal product-level instance of the promise made in the founding thesis: a new leader asking "tell me about this organization" and getting an answer built from how the org actually executes — not an org chart. Push gets a new joiner to that question sooner; pull is what actually answers it, for as long as they keep asking.

SHEET 18 / 19

XVIII. Where Intelligence Actually Sits

Worth stating plainly, once, for anyone evaluating this seriously: the system is not "AI end to end," and that is by design, not by limitation. Combining probabilistic AI at defined edges with a deterministic core is a named, standard pattern for trustworthy enterprise systems — neurosymbolic AI — not a thinned-down version of a more ambitious idea.

The Actual Split

Genuinely AI — LLM call every timeIntake's parsing agent, Capture's commitment parser, dependency extraction, override-reasoning capture, the AI Assistant / new-joiner context
Deterministic — rule-based, no model at runtimeGraph & Ledger storage, reconciliation, tolerance evaluation, the Action state machine, routing, scheduling, roll-up arithmetic, confidence scoring, RBAC checks, tracker lifecycle
The right test isn't "what fraction of the code is AI" — it's "does core value survive removing it." Remove Capture's parsing and the product reverts to the exact spreadsheet-death failure mode the founding thesis diagnosed (Sheet I). That dependency is real and structural — which is what makes this AI-native, independent of the fact that most of the engineering by volume is traditional backend work. Both are true at once; neither undercuts the other.

Compounding Intelligence — What Actually Learns

No model is retrained or fine-tuned. No weights change. What compounds is the Context Ledger, not the model — every scored cycle adds one more structured, confidence-rated precedent to a growing record the organization owns outright.

Surface historical patternDeterministic — a Ledger query and aggregate
Capture the override's reasoningAI — the same Capture mechanism, extracting a structured hypothesis from what someone says
Track indicators through the cycleDeterministic — reconciliation, as elsewhere
Score the hypothesis at cycle closeDeterministic — arithmetic against a recorded prediction, not a judgment call
Find the closest precedent next timeAI, specifically retrieval (RAG) — recognizing a new situation resembles a past one when it isn't an exact tag-match

AI's real contribution to compounding is exactly two things: capturing an override's reasoning as structured data at the moment it's said, and reasoning about which past precedent is closest when a new situation arises. Everything that makes the judgment reliable — the scoring, the confidence number, the historical aggregate — is deterministic arithmetic over data the organization already owns.

This is a stronger claim to a technical buyer than "the AI learns," not a weaker one: the organization's accumulated judgment is transparent, inspectable, and entirely theirs — sitting in their own Ledger, not locked inside a model that could behave differently after a vendor's next update.

Design Choice: Retrieval Over Fine-Tuning

Periodically fine-tuning a model on an org's own Ledger data — so the model itself absorbs organizational patterns, rather than retrieving them at query time — is a legitimate alternative architecture, and worth weighing on its merits rather than dismissing by default.

It doesn't hold up here, for several independent reasons:

  • Volume. Fine-tuning needs thousands to millions of examples to meaningfully shift behavior. A single org's scored override history, even after years of use, produces a few hundred events at most — nowhere near enough.
  • Non-determinism moves into the wrong layer. LLM generation is inherently probabilistic whether or not the model is fine-tuned — the same question can yield different phrasing or emphasis run twice. The design deliberately keeps every actual decision (confidence number, tolerance breach, routing) as deterministic arithmetic, never an LLM output. Fine-tuning "judgment" into model weights would reintroduce exactly this inconsistency risk into the part of the system that most needs to be repeatable. Retrieval avoids this because it only ever informs what's shown, never what the number is.
  • Staleness. A fine-tuned model only reflects data as of its last training run. The Ledger reflects new data the instant it's written, no retrain lag.
  • Auditability. A retrieved precedent traces to an exact past event. A fine-tuned model's output generally can't be traced to which historical cases shaped it — a real trust cost for something advising on financial commitments.
  • Multi-tenant economics. Customer execution histories are private and shouldn't cross-contaminate — fine-tuning implies one separately trained, hosted, and maintained model per customer, a cost that scales with customer count. Retrieval scales the normal SaaS way: one shared frontier-model API, isolated data per customer.
  • Catastrophic forgetting. Narrow fine-tuning on one org's execution data risks degrading the same model's general ability to correctly parse the wide variety of ways people phrase commitments in Capture.

The one place fine-tuning is a legitimate future option: distilling a small model purely for extraction speed/cost on high-volume Capture parsing — an optimization on how the AI-parsing edge runs, not a replacement for the Ledger as where intelligence lives.

XIX. Synopsis — The One-Minute Pitch

Two versions of the same story, tuned for two different questions: what does this do, and — for anyone intrigued enough to ask — what's actually under the hood.

Functional — "What is this?"

Every company already knows how to plan. What breaks is execution — the gap between deciding something in a review and actually delivering it. Right now, that gap gets managed manually, spreadsheets and someone chasing updates, and by the time a shortfall shows up on a dashboard, it's too late to fix.

EI is an AI-native execution intelligence platform that closes that gap. It learns your Annual Operating Plan — every goal, every KPI, how they cascade down to each team. When people make commitments in their normal reviews, our AI captures them straight from plain language — no forms, no re-typing into a tracker. It follows up automatically, flags when something's genuinely at risk against your real numbers, and routes it for a decision instead of letting it quietly slip.

The part that compounds: every commitment, every override, every outcome gets recorded — and AI is what lets us reason over that history, surfacing the closest precedent the moment it's relevant. Next cycle, EI doesn't just track — it knows which assumptions actually hold for your organization, and which don't.
