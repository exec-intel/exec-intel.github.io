Level 01 · The Problem · 01
The AOP is central to everything you do
AOP
ANNUAL OPERATING PLAN

Every company starts the year the same way. Before the first quarter begins, it sets an Annual Operating Plan — its ambition for the next twelve months, and the commitments it's making to get there.

FY26 · TOP-DOWN + BOTTOM-UP · COMMITTED
It comes from two directions

Top-down, the CEO's office sets the ambition. Bottom-up, every function and business builds what it believes it can commit to. The plan isn't final until both sides agree — a shared, negotiated commitment, not a number handed down.

It cascades into a goal for every team — and every person

If every team and every individual delivers their number, the company delivers its number. The AOP isn't a document sitting in a slide deck — it's a chain of accountability running from the CEO's office down to a single owner's target.

Two different kinds of work sit underneath every goal

A sales team growing accounts is BAU — it never "finishes." Acquiring and integrating a company, fixing working capital, lifting NPS — those are initiatives, with a defined outcome and a deadline. Many initiatives don't belong to one team alone.

Accountability looks different for each

For BAU, accountability sits with every individual owner, everywhere, at once. For cross-functional initiatives, one team or leader is usually named to drive it — pulling on the functions involved without owning their day-to-day work.

By the time the year begins

The ambition is set. The plan is committed, top-down and bottom-up. Every number — BAU and initiative alike — has an owner.

This is what the organization is holding itself to, for the next twelve months.

Level 01 · The Problem · 02
Fall behind on plan, can't execute well enough
Jan — the year begins

In January, plan and reality sit in the same place. The AOP is agreed, ambitious, and — on paper — achievable. What happens over the next twelve months decides whether it stays that way.

Q1 — early cracks

Nothing alarming yet — just two small slips that a Q1 review would call "manageable."

Q2 — the gaps widen

Two operational misses, tracked separately, reviewed separately — nobody's yet connected them to the same widening line.

Q3 — reacting, not recovering

The company does what most companies do: it throws a dedicated team at the problem. The team is real, the mandate is real — but so is the volume of dependencies nobody had fully mapped.

Q4 — the gap becomes the headline
24 REVIEW MEETINGS THIS QUARTER — AND COUNTING

More meetings. More action plans. Both trying to fix, in November, what quietly started slipping in February.

Twelve months later

None of this showed up in a dashboard until it was too late to change the outcome.

The plan didn't fail on paper. It failed in the months nobody was watching closely enough.

Level 02.1 · Solution · 02
What is Execution Intelligence?
The gap it's named for

Execution Intelligence is the layer that handles the coordination, follow-up, and chasing that sit between a decision getting made and an outcome actually getting delivered.

Imagine this as one workspace

Not three separate tools. At the centre, the same performance view you already know. Alongside it, an AI assistant to get the day-to-day work done. And running through both, Execution Intelligence — quietly surfacing what actually needs your attention right now.

Not a task manager. Not a dumping ground.

It focuses only on the commitments, approvals, and dependencies connected to something larger the organization is trying to achieve.

The one-line difference

Business Intelligence transformed the way organizations understand performance.

Execution Intelligence has the potential to transform the way they deliver it.

Level 02.1 · Solution · 03
How do you build it?
Context in: the plan

Nobody expects a new executive to understand the business just because they have logins to SAP and Salesforce. Someone sits down and explains it — the goals, the initiatives, how success is measured, how it all connects. The engine is taught the same way. The AOP is ingested as context before anything gets tracked, not inferred from transactions after the fact.

Context in: the commitments

Every commitment captured in a collections review is read against the plan already taught, not logged in isolation. "Amit — Client ABC, ₹1.5Cr by Friday" means something specific because the engine already knows what collections target it rolls up to.

Context reveals true impact

18% migration on its own doesn't tell you much. Once the engine knows this segment is only 10% of total receivables, the same number translates into real impact — about 3 days, not the 30 the plan assumed. Context is what turns an activity metric into a business outcome.

Context that compounds

When a plan's assumption is very different from history, EI questions management and allows them to override. Subsequently, it updates its context — and updates it again based on the actual outcome.

Level 02.2 · System's Eye View · 01
Intake
What it is

The layer where an unstructured plan becomes structured, taught context — parsed once, then written into two stores the rest of the system reads from.

How it works

An LLM extraction pass parses the source plan — deck, sheet, or doc — into initiatives, owners, targets, and dependencies. Nothing goes live until someone confirms the extracted structure.

Concrete example

Every commitment captured in a review channel becomes a Ledger event and a graph edge in the same transaction — tagged to owner and initiative at entry, not reconciled later.

Level 02.2 · System's Eye View · 02
Judgment
What it is

The layer that decides what deviation deserves attention — using thresholds the organization sets on the graph itself, not a black-box score.

How it works

A scheduled job polls the system of record and diffs it against self-reported Ledger events — self-report is a leading signal, reconciliation is the lagging check.

Concrete example

"On track" said the review. The ERP feed, two days later, disagreed by more than the initiative's tolerance allows — flagged before it became next month's surprise.

Level 02.2 · System's Eye View · 03
Action
What it is

The layer that turns a flagged deviation into a routed decision — a state machine, not a red mark sitting on a dashboard.

How it works

Breaches within the node's configured limit auto-transition. Beyond it, an approval event routes via Slack or Teams to the graph-defined owner.

Concrete example

A scheduled check compares checkpoints against the Ledger. No event by deadline auto-fires the same "unmet" transition as an explicit delay — silence is never a safe default.

Level 02.2 · System's Eye View · 04
Memory
What it is

The layer that turns a one-time override into tested, reusable judgment. This is what makes the system compound — not a retrained model, a scored, structured record.

How it works

An override isn't just accepted — it's written as a conditional edge linking the historical base rate to the new claimed condition, scored at cycle close against what actually happened.

Concrete example

A single validated cycle nudges confidence, it doesn't settle it. The score keeps moving with repetition — real evidence, shown as a number, not a claim.

Level 02.2 · System's Eye View · 05
How it all connects
The full loop

One continuous loop — the plan comes in once, every commitment and outcome writes back into the same graph, and every cycle sharpens what the next one starts with.

What it connects to

Four real connections, not an abstraction: the system of record for reconciliation, the CRM for account context, chat for capture and nudges, and a model-agnostic LLM layer for parsing. Each one is genuine integration work — not a plug-in.

Agents vs. deterministic jobs

Only one step genuinely needs an LLM — reading an unstructured plan. Everything after that is deterministic: scheduled jobs, threshold checks, routing rules. Auditable by design, not a black box end to end.

This isn't plug-and-play, and it shouldn't pretend to be.

One time integration effort — a system that gets sharper every cycle after.

Level 03 · Illustration · 01
Collections
The commitment

A BU is lagging on collections despite contracted terms. The leader convenes a review. Account by account, the team commits — captured in natural language, not a spreadsheet — until it adds up to the target, due in 9 days.

The exception

Mid-cycle, one commitment slips. The owner types the update as it happens — not a silent miss, an explained one. But it's enough to put the ₹45Cr target at risk.

The approval

Because the delay breaches tolerance, it routes for a decision — not silently absorbed, not left for the next review. The manager sees the full picture and approves. The tracker updates immediately.

Reconciliation and roll-up

In parallel, actual cash received in the ERP is checked against what's reported. Once confirmed, this BU's numbers roll up automatically into the company-wide view — no one re-keys it twice.

Level 03 · Illustration · 02
PMI
The plan — XYZ Inc → ABC Inc

A cross-functional PMI team is stood up for the XYZ Inc → ABC Inc integration. Five tracks, five owners, one shared 90-day clock.

Status check-in

Owners report status in plain language, the same way the collections team does — not a form, a sentence. Two tracks are already flashing.

Learning from history

At kickoff, management overrode the historical pattern with a specific reason. The engine logged it as a hypothesis, not a fact — and now, checked against what's actually happening, it isn't holding up.

Risk surfaces

A delayed status update isn't just a date slipping — the system quantifies what it actually costs. Two unbudgeted impacts surface, totaling roughly $240,000.

Routed to the PMI lead

All three signals route to the person who owns the integration outcome, not back to the teams that surfaced them — two budget risks and one assumption that didn't hold. One view, the full picture, before it becomes next quarter's surprise.

Level 03 · Illustration · 03
Sales Performance
The high-level dashboard

Freight Forwarding outperforms on existing clients, underperforms on new — overall still good given its size. Truck Load lags on both. Domestic Courier is solid on both. Warehousing, the new offering, hasn't taken off.

Bottom-up: new client acquisition

Underneath the headline numbers, every line is short on new client acquisition — not just Warehousing. The overall picture looks fine only because existing accounts are making up the difference.

The bridge commitment

The Warehousing team names the real cause — longer cycles than modeled — and commits to bridging the shortfall through cross-sell into existing accounts. The company-level gap closes. Warehousing, on its own, doesn't.

Reviewed later

The cross-sell commitment is largely holding — but only partially. Most of the gap has closed; some hasn't. The residual stays visible and tracked, not quietly absorbed into "close enough."
